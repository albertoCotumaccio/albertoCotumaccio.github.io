In recent months, I decided to follow a path I would have never associated with my daily work: **MBSR – Mindfulness Based Stress Reduction** ([Mindfulness](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mindfulness)).  
My days are usually marked by code, technical issues, meetings, and deadlines. Mindfulness appeared at first as something distant, yet I wanted to give it space and see whether it could have a real impact, even in a technical and fast-paced environment.

Over time, I began to see its value not in abstract terms, but in practical ones. It is not about seeking special moments or particular states, but about observing more clearly what happens, without automatically falling back into established habits. This mindset has influenced the way I work and organize my tasks ([“Research: When Mindfulness Does — and Doesn’t — Help at Work”](https://hbr.org/2022/12/research-when-mindfulness-does-and-doesnt-help-at-work)).

---

## The story of Amonte and Avalle

During the course, I came across a meaningful story: **Amonte and Avalle**.  
Avalle is a village crossed by a river. Every day, the inhabitants see bodies drifting downstream. Over time, they built efficient rescue teams and well-organized hospitals. But no one ever thought of going upstream to investigate the origin of the problem.

I find this a fitting metaphor for technical work: we are often fast and effective at solving emergencies, but we don’t always stop to reflect on root causes. In this sense, mindfulness is an invitation to step back and analyze the broader context instead of remaining in reactive mode.

---

## What mindfulness is

The definition given by Jon Kabat-Zinn was the cornerstone of the course:

> “Paying attention in a particular way: on purpose, in the present moment, and non-judgmentally.”

It is not relaxation, nor escaping thoughts. It is a method for observing what happens without judgment, distinguishing between direct experience and the interpretations we automatically add. The practices explored during the course included:

* **Breath meditation**  
* **Body Scan**  
* **Sound awareness**  
* **Observation of thoughts**

These exercises highlight how often we move without awareness, jumping from one activity to another without really being present. Scientific literature shows that regular practice can improve stress management, concentration, and overall well-being ([Effects of Mindfulness on Psychological Health: A Review of Empirical Studies](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC3679190/)).

## An experiment in software development

To evaluate whether practice had an impact on my work, I took part in four **bug fixing** sessions under the following conditions:

* 1 hour per session  
* 6 hidden bugs  
* Java code on Git  
* Different scenarios: Pac-Man, an event system, a vending machine, a calculator  

The sessions took place at different times: before the course, right after, two months later, and again some months after. Productivity alone was not being observed: during the sessions we also wore **biometric wristbands** that tracked data such as heartbeat, heart rate variability, and galvanic skin response. These data were later analyzed to measure stress levels and physiological variations. The aim was not only to evaluate performance, but also how psychophysiological responses changed.

---

## Observed results

Some recurring aspects emerged over time:

* **More continuity of attention**: less dispersion and easier return to the task.  
* **Awareness of working conditions**: recognizing when it was useful to pause or shift perspective.  
* **Clearer distinction between thoughts and reality**: reducing the impact of personal interpretations during problem-solving.  
* **More strategic use of breaks**: short pauses to maintain clarity and stability in the process.  

These findings are consistent with recent studies linking mindfulness to improvements in executive functions and emotional regulation.

---

## Practices that remain useful

* **Body Scan** as a re-centering exercise.  
* **Breath meditation** as a simple reference point.  
* **Walking meditation** as an opportunity for awareness even in movement.  
* **Mindful routines**: treating common actions as opportunities for presence.  

---

## Mindfulness and technical work

Software development constantly requires balancing detail and big-picture thinking. Mindfulness works here as training to keep both levels of attention active: precision on a single line of code and awareness of the overall architectural context. This parallel feels concrete to me: the same mental discipline that is useful in meditation applies to addressing complex and collaborative problems.

---

The MBSR program has ended, but what remains is the possibility of applying these practices at any moment. There is no need to add extra hours to the day: it is about changing the quality of the time already available.

At the end of the course, the instructor said: *“Meditation is not for the faint of heart.”*  
I see this as a direct invitation to continue with consistency, seriousness, and curiosity — just like when starting a new complex project.
